About me

Hello, I'm Lenka.

I grew up surrounded by the lush, layered landscapes of Slovakia — a country where wild forests meet cultivated meadows, and where every village garden tells a story of the seasons.

After studying landscape architecture in Bratislava and working on residential projects across Central Europe, I made the move to the United Kingdom with one mission: to bring that sense of natural abundance and thoughtful structure into British outdoor spaces.

UK gardens are full of potential, yet so many feel trapped in the same formula — a lawn, a border, a patio. I want to show you what's possible when we draw inspiration from a different tradition: layered planting, living structures, edible landscapes, and gardens that change beautifully with every season.

A labour of love

My Slovakia Garden — A 5-Year Journey

Before redesigning gardens for others, I redesigned my own. This is the story of the family garden in Banská Bystrica — from a bare patch of grass to a layered, seasonal paradise.

Spring 2018

The Blank Canvas

When we bought the property, the garden was a flat lawn surrounded by overgrown conifer hedges. Beautiful bones — but zero character. I spent the first season just observing: where the sun falls, where water collects, which corner smells of the forest in the morning.

  • Survey
  • Soil analysis
  • Observation
Autumn 2019

Structure & Bones

Year two was about structure. We removed the conifers and replaced them with a mixed native hedgerow. Curved stone paths were laid using local Carpathian sandstone. Three large raised beds were built from larch timber for the kitchen garden.

  • Pathways
  • Raised beds
  • Hedgerow planting
Summer 2020

The Meadow Experiment

The lawn on the south-facing slope was converted to a wildflower meadow using a mix of native Slovak species: cornflower, field poppy, ox-eye daisy and knapweed. By July it was buzzing with bees and beetles. My neighbours thought I'd lost my mind — until they saw it in bloom.

  • Wildflower meadow
  • Native species
  • Biodiversity
Spring 2021

The Forest Garden Layer

Three apple trees, two pear trees and a plum were planted to form the upper canopy. Beneath them: hazel, elder and gooseberry for the shrub layer. Comfrey, sweet cicely and ground ivy for the ground cover. This is permaculture thinking — seven layers of productivity in a space smaller than a tennis court.

  • Fruit trees
  • Forest garden
  • Permaculture
Autumn 2022

Water & Reflection

A small natural pond was dug into the lowest point of the garden — where water was already collecting after heavy rain anyway. It's lined with clay, edged with local stone and planted with yellow flag iris, water mint and common reed. Within one season it had frogs, dragonflies and a pair of visiting mallards.

  • Natural pond
  • Water planting
  • Wildlife habitat
Summer 2023

The Garden in Full Voice

Five years later, the garden is barely recognisable from the blank lawn we started with. The hedgerow has knitted together into a solid boundary. The meadow self-seeds. The fruit trees are fruiting. And the pond is alive. This is what I want to bring to every garden I work in — life, layers, and a sense that the garden is growing with you, not just sitting there.

  • Maturity
  • Self-sustaining
  • Complete ecosystem
